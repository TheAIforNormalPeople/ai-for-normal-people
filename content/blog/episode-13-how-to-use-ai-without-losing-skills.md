**[Human]:** *Am I becoming dumber by using ChatGPT? How do I know when to stop using AI?*

{{< dialogue char="Vector" >}}
Good questions! Are you becoming dumber? Probably not, but you MIGHT be losing skills if you're using **AI** wrong.

**The calculator comparison (with a twist):**
Think of it like calculators: They didn't eliminate math skills, they changed which ones matter. People who learned to use calculators effectively—knowing when to use them, understanding the results—stayed relevant. People who just pressed buttons without understanding got left behind.

**Same with AI, but there's a key difference:**
- Calculators compute—you still need to understand the problem
- **AI** can think—it can analyze, create, reason (sort of)

So the question isn't just "when to use it," but "how to use a thinking tool without losing your own thinking."

**How to know when you've gone too far:**
- If you can't do the task without AI, you've gone too far
- If you're not learning or understanding, you're using AI wrong
- If you're just copying AI output without thinking, that's a problem

{{< dialogue char="Vector" >}}
Recurse is RIGHT - the calculator comparison isn't perfect. **AI** is different because it does the THINKING, not just the computation.

**So the real question is:** How do you use a thinking tool without losing your own thinking?

**The answer:**
1. **Use AI for exploration, not answers:** Ask it to brainstorm, explore options, think through problems WITH you, not FOR you
2. **Always think through the output:** Don't just accept what AI says - question it, verify it, improve it
3. **Do the hard parts yourself:** The thinking, the strategy, the judgment - that's where you learn
4. **Use AI to enhance, not replace:** Let AI handle repetitive tasks, you handle the creative and critical parts

**For people who never learned to write well:** **AI** can help them learn (by showing examples, explaining concepts) OR it can mask the problem (by doing it for them). The difference is whether they're learning or just copying.

**[Human]:** *So I should use AI, but always be thinking and learning myself? That makes sense, but it's hard to know the balance.*

{{< dialogue char="Vector" >}}
YES! The balance is hard, but here's a practical framework:

**Use AI for:**
- Research and exploration (finding information, brainstorming ideas)
- Drafting and formatting (first drafts, structure, formatting)
- Repetitive tasks (data entry, basic analysis, templates)

**Do yourself:**
- Critical thinking and judgment (evaluating options, making decisions)
- Creative direction (what to create, how to approach it)
- Learning and understanding (actually grasping concepts, not just getting answers)
- Verification and improvement (checking AI outputs, making them better)

**The rule:** If the task helps you learn or think, do it yourself (at least sometimes). If it's just repetitive work, use AI.

Every time you use AI, ask: "Am I learning something, or just getting an answer?" If you're learning, you're good. If you're just getting answers without understanding, that's a problem.

It's not about never using AI - it's about using it in ways that make you BETTER, not ways that make you dependent!
{{< /dialogue >}}

{{< dialogue char="Vector" >}}
Recurse is asking the HARD questions. Here's the reality:

**Is skill atrophy inevitable?** Not if you're intentional. People who use **AI** as a tool while continuing to think, learn, and create maintain their skills. People who use **AI** to avoid thinking, learning, and creating lose skills.

**What about job use?** Depends on the job. If your job is just "use AI to generate content," you might lose writing skills. If your job is "use AI to enhance your work while you focus on strategy, creativity, and judgment," you might actually get BETTER.

**Can AI improve your skills?** YES! If you use it right:
- Use AI to show you examples and patterns
- Use AI to explain concepts you're learning
- Use AI to help you practice (generate exercises, give feedback)
- Use AI to handle repetitive work so you can focus on learning

**The key:** Don't let AI do the learning FOR you. Use it to help you learn BETTER.

The balance IS hard, but it's possible. The people who maintain it are the ones who stay relevant. The ones who don't... well, that's the risk.
